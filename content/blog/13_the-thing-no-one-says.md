# the thing no one says

there's a meeting i've been in a hundred times. different rooms, different teams, different stakes. but the same shape.

someone presents options. the group discusses. people ask questions, offer considerations, nod at the right moments. and then a decision gets made — or, more often, deferred — and everyone leaves.

and the actual reason the decision was hard doesn't get named once.

---

sometimes the thing no one says is about someone's role. there's a person in the room whose territory gets smaller if option A is chosen, and everyone knows it, and that dynamic is shaping every contribution they make, but no one's going to name that in the meeting.

sometimes it's about a relationship. two stakeholders have an unresolved tension that predates this decision by eighteen months, and the current disagreement is a proxy for that older one.

sometimes it's simpler: someone made a commitment three months ago that this decision would implicitly undo, and admitting that means admitting the commitment was premature.

none of these are unusual. they're just things that happen when decisions involve people. the problem isn't that they exist. it's that they're doing work in the room while pretending they're not there.

---

i've learned to listen for certain signals.

when people keep circling back to the same objection even after it's been addressed — usually means the objection is pointing at something that isn't the stated objection. when someone goes quiet who's normally vocal, something shifted. when a decision keeps getting deferred even though the information's available — the information isn't the actual hold-up.

none of this is complicated to notice once you're looking for it. the trick is that most meetings aren't designed to surface it. they're designed to surface information, evaluate options, reach alignment. the subtext is treated as irrelevant or inappropriate, so it never gets a clean channel.

which means it keeps running in the background.

---

the decisions i've seen go most sideways aren't the ones where the options were wrong. they're the ones where the real constraint — the one no one said — quietly shaped everything and then wasn't accounted for in the outcome.

the team commits to option B, but the person who's going to implement it never actually bought in. six weeks later, it's stalled. the stakeholders are confused. but if you'd been in that room listening for the right things, you'd have seen it coming.

the only way i've found to address this is to make it okay to name. not in a therapy-session way. just: is there anything that's making this hard to decide that we haven't said yet? sometimes people say no. sometimes someone says the thing that's been sitting in the middle of the table for an hour.

that one sentence changes the whole meeting.

---

it doesn't always need to be named explicitly. sometimes just knowing it's there is enough — you structure the discussion differently, give certain people room to speak first, frame the options in a way that reduces the stakes of the unspoken thing.

but you can only do any of that if you've registered it. and most people haven't built the habit of listening for it.
