# design is an argument

i used to think my job was to make things beautiful. then i realised the beautiful things that worked were the ones where i had a clear opinion about what was true.

design is not decoration. it's not "making things look nice." it's a series of claims about how people work, what they need, and what's worth their attention. every layout is a hypothesis. every colour choice is a bet on what matters.

the problem is that most design processes are built to avoid this. consensus-seeking. "can we try it both ways?" endless iteration that moves sideways instead of forward. we call it collaboration. sometimes it is. often it's just not wanting to be wrong in public.

being wrong in public is the job.

a wireframe that says "this is the structure and here's why" invites real feedback. a wireframe that says "here are three options" invites people to pick their favourite. those are different conversations and they produce different outcomes.

i'm not talking about being stubborn. i'm talking about knowing what you're arguing for before you walk in the room. what does this design make easy? what does it make hard? what does it assume about the person using it? if you can't answer those, you haven't designed anything. you've produced output.

the best feedback i ever got was from a PM who said "i don't understand why this is the shape it is." not "this is wrong" — just a genuine question about the reasoning. that was the moment i realised i'd been hiding behind the artefact. the design was my answer but i hadn't shown my working.

show your working.

make the argument. be specific about what you're claiming and why. then let people push back on the claim — not just the aesthetics, not just their gut, but the actual position.

that's what design critique should be. not "i prefer the other version" but "i think you're wrong about this" — and then a real conversation about who's right and what evidence would settle it.

design that can't be argued with can't be improved.
