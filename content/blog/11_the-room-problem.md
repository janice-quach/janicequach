# the room problem

i've walked out of presentations thinking the work was good, the room was wrong, and the problem was them.

i've also walked out knowing the work was good and the problem was me. the gap between those two assessments is most of what i've learned about presenting.

there's a version of design culture that treats "having to sell your work" as a failure of the process. if the design is right, people should be able to see it. if they can't see it, that's a taste problem or a politics problem and not really yours to solve.

i used to believe something close to this. i don't anymore.

the room doesn't know what you know. you've spent weeks with the research, the edge cases, the versions that didn't work. they've had thirty seconds with a deck. the gap between those two starting points is real, and bridging it is design work — not compromise, not politics, actual communication design.

what i've noticed is that most rooms don't fail on the design. they fail on the frame.

if people don't know what question you were trying to answer, they'll project their own question onto your work and evaluate it against that. if they don't understand what constraints you were working within, they'll suggest solutions you already tried. if they don't know what you're asking of them — decision, feedback, buy-in — they'll default to giving you the one thing most rooms default to giving: notes.

a presentation that starts with "here's what we built" is making the audience do work you should have done. a presentation that starts with "here's the problem, here's what makes it hard, here's what we tried, here's what we landed on and why" gives them somewhere to stand.

the other thing — and this one took me longer to learn — is that a room that doesn't move is usually a room that doesn't feel heard. not heard on the final design, necessarily, but heard somewhere earlier. if someone raised a concern three weeks ago and they don't see any trace of it in the work, they'll raise it again, louder. that's not obstruction, that's just how it feels to have your thinking ignored.

i try to show my working now. not because the process justifies the outcome but because it makes the outcome legible. "we considered X and didn't pursue it because Y" is not defensive — it's information. it closes the loop on the concerns that got here before the work did.

there are still rooms that won't move no matter what you do. bad conditions, wrong moment, stakeholder agendas that have nothing to do with your presentation. you can't solve for those.

but most rooms that don't move had a solvable problem. and it was usually set up before the first slide.
