# what alignment actually means

alignment is one of those words that gets used constantly in design work and almost never defined.

you hear it after a workshop: "i think we're aligned now." you hear it as a blocker: "we need more alignment before we can move forward." you hear it as a goal: "the purpose of this meeting is to get aligned."

and if you asked everyone in the room what they meant by it, you'd get different answers. which is the problem.

---

i've started to think there are at least three different things people mean when they say alignment.

the first is agreement. everyone saying yes to the same option. this is the version people usually mean when they say "we need to align," and it's the least useful definition. agreement can be manufactured — enough meetings, enough pressure, enough wanting to move on. agreement that came from attrition isn't alignment. it just looks like it until implementation starts.

the second is shared understanding. everyone having the same mental model of the problem, the constraints, the tradeoffs. this is harder to reach than agreement and much more durable. teams with shared understanding will make consistent decisions even when they're not in the room together. teams with surface agreement will diverge the moment the specifics come up.

the third is commitment. people actually willing to act on the decision, not just assenting to it. this is the version that's most invisible in meetings and most consequential in execution. you can have agreement and shared understanding and still have a room full of people who aren't going to follow through — because the decision didn't actually resolve their concern, it just moved past it.

most "alignment failures" i've seen in practice aren't disagreements. they're cases where teams had agreement without understanding, or understanding without commitment. the decision looked solid in the room and fell apart in the doing.

---

the way i've learned to check for this is to ask what people are going to do differently as a result of the meeting.

not "are we aligned?" that question has one answer: yes. people are not going to say no in a meeting where everyone else is nodding.

but "what does this mean for your next two weeks?" is a real question. if four people give you four different answers, you haven't aligned on anything actionable. if someone says "i'm not sure how this changes what i'm working on," that's a signal that the commitment piece isn't there yet.

it's a small shift. it just requires being willing to hear the answer.

---

alignment also isn't a moment. it's a state that has to be maintained.

the team that was aligned in january is not necessarily aligned in march. context changes. priorities shift. new information arrives. and because "we already aligned on this" is treated as a reason not to revisit, teams often operate on decisions that quietly became wrong weeks ago.

the work of maintaining alignment is the work of keeping shared understanding current. not relitigating every decision, but being willing to surface when the decision's underlying assumptions have changed. that requires someone whose job it is to notice — and most teams don't assign that job to anyone explicitly, which means nobody does it.
